THE HIDDEN VICTIMS

OF

CLIMATE CHANGE

  • THE CRISIS

    Forcing millions from their homes as a result of floods, droughts, storms, and rising sea levels, climate change is increasingly affecting people and regions across the globe. Patterns of migration towards urban cities and overseas have induced drastic changes in demographic composition, government policy, economic balance, and resource expenditure on a global scale. Legislation to reduce carbon emissions has been a hot topic in the U.S. Congress and in governments worldwide. But there is another climate crisis going on that has slipped behind the radar.


    As of 2015, environmental disasters have resulted in over 25 million climate refugees, with small and local communities feeling the largest impact to their populations. What was first a concern for small island nations has evolved into a larger crisis that reaches beyond national borders. Climate migration is estimated to impact more than 200 million people by 2050, a daunting number for numerous governments who already have corrupt, oftentimes uncontrollable, refugee policy. The victims of the climate refugee crisis cannot remain hidden. They must be acknowledged, heard, and aided on a global scale.

  • THE NEEDS

    The effects of climate change are only expected to worsen, and the number of refugees will continue to grow. Current greenhouse gas emissions are at their peak in human history, and if no substantive actions are taken, Earth’s average surface temperature is predicted to increase by more than 3 degrees Celsius in the next hundred years.

    The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) defines refugees as “people fleeing conflict or persecution,” but acknowledges that many people “in need of protection and assistance” do not fit this description. International protections for climate refugees will be nearly impossible to obtain for as long as they are not officially recognized by the world’s most important refugee agency.

    There is a general lack of awareness of the extent and urgency of the climate refugee crisis among government officials and the public, both in the U.S. and abroad.

  • THE PEOPLE

    In 2007, a fisherman named Ioane Teitiota left his flooded town in Kiribati and moved to New Zealand. He had no choice, he said, but to escape the effects of climate change that would soon render his home completely uninhabitable. Teitiota was arguably the first official climate change refugee. However, he was caught in New Zealand in September of 2015 for overstaying his visa, and was soon deported; there was no legal basis to grant him refugee status. The high court’s rejection reads, “Traditionally a refugee is fleeing his own government and or a non-state actor from whom the government is unwilling or unable to protect him. Thus the claimant is seeking refuge within the very countries that are allegedly ‘persecuting’ him.” When no one can be blamed but the entire human race, the court seems to be saying, no one can be held responsible.

    Teitiota situation is far from an isolated one. Farmers in México and Nigeria, fast-paced technology professionals in Beijing, and Native Americans in Louisiana reservations are facing many of the same challenges. They may decide to leave home, but they almost always have no guarantee that they’ll be accepted somewhere else. Climate refugees can be considered a subset of a more universally recognized group, “environmental refugees,” which includes those affected by any kind of natural disaster regardless of a connection to climate change. Ever since the United Nations Refugee Convention of 1951, such refugees haven’t qualified for any international protections or provisions. Millions of them are left stranded, global wanderers with no real home.

  • THE SOLUTIONS

    One solution would be legislative action on local, national, and international scales to slow the progression of climate change and mitigate its effects will also contribute to solving the climate refugee crisis. International agreements such as the 2015 Paris Agreement are steps in the right direction.

    In addition, various non-governmental organizations and nonprofit groups should work to inform and persuade the U.N. to outline provisions and protections for climate refugees. A few such organizations based in the Washington, D.C. area are Refugees International and the Center for Environmental Law.

    Government officials pay attention to the issues that are most important to their constituents, therefore, we have the ability to advocate for climate refugees as an issue that needs to be addressed. In the U.S. Congress, refugee issues are under the jurisdiction of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, specifically the Subcommittee on Immigration and the National Interest, and the House Judiciary Committee, Subcommittee on Immigration and Border Security. Members of Congress can be contacted via phone or email.
